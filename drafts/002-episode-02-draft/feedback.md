CRITICAL STORYTELLING PROBLEMS
Problem 1: Emotional Flatness (Language-Level Issue)

The outline reads like a TV show treatment—functional but bloodless. King writes: "Language is the foundation of storytelling." This outline has plot beats but lacks sensory imprint.

Example:

"Doogan clocks graffiti: 'Julio ♥ Dominique.' Nice of him to declare that but how was she supposed to see it...in here?"

This is a joke placeholder, not prose. The holding cell scene needs:

Physical sensation (metal bench cold through his shirt, smell of industrial cleaner and piss)
Emotional imprint (exhaustion, gallows humor as defense mechanism)
Martinez's voice landing like a judge's gavel
Fix Strategy:
When implementing, each scene needs King's sensory realism. The outline is your fossil excavation site. Now brush away the dirt and reveal the living tissue underneath.

Problem 2: Character Motivation Gaps

Cheryl's Reveal Timing:
Scene 16 (rehearsal dinner) delivers the emotional payload: Cheryl dumped Brad, Gabby swooped in. This is the real job—emotional containment, not distraction.

Issue: The outline doesn't show Doogan discovering this during the hunt when it would add emotional texture. He's just executing a contract until Scene 16 drops the reveal.

King's Principle: "Stories are discovered, not engineered." Cheryl's wounded pride and competitive fury should bleed through the scavenger hunt scenes. Small tells. Sharp comments. Doogan reading between lines.

Fix: Add micro-beats in Scenes 7, 9, 11, 13 where Cheryl's emotional rawness shows:

She's not just competitive—she's proving something
Comments about "younger sisters" or "weddings" have an edge
Doogan notices but doesn't press (he's too good for that)
By Scene 16, the reveal should feel inevitable, not surprising.

Problem 3: Eddie's Motor-Mouth Voice Not Landing in Outline

Eddie's character file specifies "Motor-Mouth Dialogue Rules":

Often mid-sentence when scenes begin
Steamrolls, uses em-dashes
Tangents that circle back
The Problem:
Outline beats like "Eddie: Hank costs money; money means jobs" are too clean. Eddie doesn't deliver information in crisp sentences—he avalanches.

Episode 1 Evidence (Scene 3):

"There he is! Fresh from a broken heart, our hero, food's getting cold, we got pad thai, we got drunken noodles, we got spring rolls—Abby insisted on the spring rolls even though nobody ever eats the spring rolls, but that's a whole separate conversation we don't need to have right now."

Fix:
Scene 3 (Eddie's Kitchen) needs Eddie steamrolling Doogan's jail trauma with job details:

"—so Hank costs money, obviously, guy's a lawyer even if he's a bottom-tier one, and lawyers don't work for free or friendship or any of the things normal humans accept, which means we need money, which means you need to work, which I know sounds insane considering you were literally arrested last night but guess what, the Billings still want their wedding weekend handled—"

Problem 4: Martinez Surveillance Thread Feels Tacked On

Scene 17 (Martinez Ambush) has Martinez confront Doogan about Eddie/Baxter/Abby investigating the clinic.

The Problem:
This reveal happens after the rehearsal dinner in a separate scene. It reads like procedural housekeeping, not organic character conflict.

King's Principle: "Every scene must open a door and close another."

Better Structure:
Move Martinez's surveillance reveal into Scene 16 (rehearsal dinner):

Doogan walks Cheryl to her car
She's about to invite him in
Doogan spots Martinez's car (surveillance)
Martinez approaches then—in front of Cheryl
Cheryl reads it as cop/trouble and pulls back
Doogan loses his post-job window with Cheryl
Martinez's timing becomes character—he's deliberate, pressuring Doogan socially
This way:

Martinez's reveal costs Doogan something (Cheryl connection)
Scene has two emotional beats (rehearsal dinner success + surveillance exposure)
Scene 17 becomes redundant—cut it, tighten the episode
Problem 5: Baxter's IT Infiltration Fail Lacks Stakes

Scene 10 (Baxter's clinic attempt) is played for comedy: he gets escorted out immediately.

The Problem:
It's too low-stakes. No consequences. Just "oh well, that didn't work."

King's Rewrite Principle: "Cut 10–20%." This scene is filler disguised as comedy.

Better Approach:
Combine Scenes 10 and 12 into one escalating montage:

Baxter's failed IT attempt
Eddie's failed charm attempt
Eddie's failed distraction + photo (cat videos)
All building to: "Okay. Abby goes in."
Make it one compressed sequence (Scene 10) instead of two separate beats. Tighter, funnier, more momentum.

Problem 6: Scavenger Hunt Clue Mechanics Unclear

The outline has great clue concepts (Shakespeare, Mockingbird, Gatsby, Harry Potter) but the mechanisms feel vague.

Example (Scene 7 - Flower Shop):

"I have seen roses damasked, red and white" → Doogan orders mixed roses → gets next clue

Question: How does ordering roses deliver the clue? Does the clerk hand them an envelope? Is it written on the receipt? Hidden in the bouquet?

King's Language Rule: Be specific. Vague mechanics create vague prose.

Fix:
Each hunt stop needs a clear trigger:

Flower shop: clerk asks "Any color preference?" Doogan says "Red and white," clerk smiles, hands them an envelope from under the counter
Pub: bartender offers champagne, Doogan declines, bartender nods and slides coaster with clue written underneath
Bookstore: clerk directs them to something else (not P&P), leading to Gatsby connection
Specificity creates confident prose.
