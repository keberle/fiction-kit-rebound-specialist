# Episode 2 Craft Review — Scenes 1-7
**Date:** December 26, 2025  
**Reviewer:** Stephen King Mode Agent  
**Scope:** Content/episodes/episode-02-the-wedding-weekend (Scenes 1-7)

---

## Overall Assessment

**The Good:**  
The language is clean and moves. You've got momentum. The pacing works. The scenes cut at the right moments—no lingering, no throat-clearing. The interrogation scene is sharp. The banter between Eddie and Doogan lands. The scavenger hunt setup has energy.

**The Problem:**  
You're writing around the edges of things instead of into them. The prose is competent, but it's safe. It needs teeth. It needs sensory grit. It needs emotional specificity. King doesn't write clean crime fiction—he writes about what it *feels like* to be in the room. That's missing here.

---

## Scene-by-Scene Analysis

### Scene 1: Holding Cell Graffiti

**What Works:**
- Opening line is strong: "Two hours in a holding cell and Doogan had memorized the graffiti."
- Julio ♥ Dominique detail is good—shows POV character's mind working
- Interrogation dialogue is natural, moves well
- Martinez/Finch dynamic is clear without exposition

**What Needs Work:**

**Language Issues:**
- "Fluorescent hallway that hummed too loud" — passive construction. Make it active: "The fluorescent lights hummed, too loud."
- "His legs ached from standing" — tells, doesn't show. What do aching legs *feel like*? Knees locked? Thighs burning? Weight shifting?
- "The chair was cold through his jeans" — better, but still surface. How cold? Shock of metal? Numbing cold?

**Missing Sensory Detail:**
The holding cell has "no furniture" and smells like "industrial cleaner trying to kill something older and worse." That's good. But what *is* that older smell? Sweat? Fear? Vomit? Be specific. King would name it.

**Emotional Distance:**
Doogan's been in a holding cell for two hours and is now being interrogated for murder. Where's the fear? The anger? The exhaustion pressing into every thought? You mention exhaustion at the end but it's abstract. Make it physical. Make it *hurt*.

**Mechanical Problem:**
The wine bottle disappearing is a critical plot point, but Doogan's reaction is too muted. He says "I don't know. It was there." That's passive. This should be alarming—someone moved evidence from a crime scene. His pulse should spike. His mind should race. Show that.

**Dialogue Weakness:**
- "You want me to lie and say I'm a cat person?" — too quippy for the situation
- "I think...I blacked out before anything happened" — the ellipsis and the casual delivery undercut the revelation

**Cut This:**
- "Nice of Julio to declare that. Real romantic. But how was Dominique supposed to see it in here?" — First line is good. The rest is over-explaining the joke. Trust the reader.

### Scene 2: The Walkout

**What Works:**
- Eddie's run-on opening works for his character
- Hank Stillman character sketch is efficient: "business card...with the practiced efficiency of a parking meter dispensing a receipt"
- Lawyer scene moves, gets necessary info in without bogging down
- Abby's "pancakes and bail money" line lands

**What Needs Work:**

**Opening Violation (McDonald Rule):**
Scene 1 ends with Doogan saying he blacked out. Scene 2 opens with Finch talking about lawyers. That's fine. But then we're in hallway logistics for too long before anything changes. Jump faster to the complication (Hank's cost, the earpiece being taken).


**Adverb Alert:**
- "already walking away" — cut "already"
- "already moving on" — cut "already"
- Multiple instances of "already" throughout. You're using it as rhythm filler. Cut them all.

**Missing Conflict:**
When Hank says $15,000, Doogan should react more. That's real money. He's just been accused of murder. His best friend is about to pay it. Where's the gut-punch?

### Scene 3: Eddie's Kitchen

**What Works:**
- Eddie's opening run-on sentence is perfectly in character
- Abby's "You look like hell" / "I'm being generous" — sharp, lands
- Pancake detail (butter melting) is specific
- Doogan "barely registered the flavor" is good psychological detail
- Earpiece discussion is handled efficiently without over-explaining

**What Needs Work:**

**Language Problem:**
"Abby stood at the stove, flipping pancakes with the kind of precision that suggested she'd rather be doing anything else."

This is telling masquerading as showing. How does precision suggest reluctance? Show the reluctance: sharp flips, impatient scrape of spatula, tight mouth.

**Repetition:**
You use "Eddie was mid-sentence when..." twice in two scenes (Scene 2 and Scene 3). Pattern is starting to show. Vary.

**Sensory Absence:**
The pancakes "smelled good" — too vague. What do pancakes smell like? Butter? Vanilla? Warmth? Be specific.

**Dialogue Issue:**
Abby says: "You blacked out...Before anything happened...That's not normal...The wine...Probably."

This exchange is too efficient. Reads like shorthand. Real people would circle this conversation more—it's about drugging, potential rape, murder. The stakes deserve more weight.

**Missing Physical:**
Doogan's exhausted. He's been up for 30+ hours. He was arrested. Where's the crash? His hands should shake. His vision should blur. His body should be *screaming* at him. You mention it abstractly but don't make us feel it.

### Scene 4: Doogan's Condo

**What Works:**
- "immaculate, staged, empty" — three words that tell us everything about how Doogan lives
- Shower scene internal monologue is tight
- Baxter's practical approach to the earpiece replacement
- Scene is short, doesn't overstay

**What Needs Work:**

**Missed Opportunity:**
"The shower was hot. He stood under it longer than necessary, letting the water run over him, trying to wash away the smell of industrial cleaner and interrogation rooms and exhaustion."

This is where you go deeper. This is where Doogan's alone and the walls come down. What does he *feel*? Is he scared? Angry? What's his body doing? Is he shaking? Are his hands steady? This is the emotional core of the episode and you're skating past it.

**Language:**
- "trying to wash away" — weak verb. Water can't wash away exhaustion. Be more specific about what he's doing.
- "He'd been in bad situations before" — tells. If we need to know this, show it through his physical reaction.

**No Sensory Detail:**
Water temperature. Water pressure. Steam. Soap smell. Tile texture. Give us *something* physical to anchor to.

**The Setup:**
"Someone had studied him. Learned his patterns. Set him up perfectly."

This is revelation-level thinking. But it's delivered flat. Where's the gut-punch? The fear? The rage?

**What Works:**
- Thomas correcting "Tom" is efficient character work

**Opening (Again):**
Another "Eddie was mid-pitch when..." opening. Third time. It's becoming a crutch.

He's 24 hours past arrest. He should be a wreck. You mention it ("The exhaustion was still there—bone-deep") but then he has sharp, clear exchanges with the Billings. Where's the fog? The lag in response? The effort to seem functional?

**Missing Scene Structure:**
What's the turn? The information comes out but nothing *changes*. They agree to the job at the start, and at the end they've... agreed to the job. No complication. No surprise.

## Craft Issues (King Principles)

### 1. Adverb Addiction

Count your adverbs ending in -ly:
- "slightly" (used 6 times)
- "already" (used 11 times)
- "exactly" (used 4 times)
- "perfectly" (used 5 times)

Cut them. All of them. Find stronger verbs or restructure sentences.

**Example:**
"Martinez leaned forward slightly."  
→ "Martinez leaned in."

### 2. Weak Verbs

You default to weak verbs + adverbs instead of strong verbs:
- "looked quickly" → "glanced"
- "moved slowly" → "crept" / "dragged"
- "said quietly" → "murmured" / "whispered"

### 3. Passive Voice

Too many passive constructions:
- "His legs ached from standing" → "Standing had welded his knees together"
- "The exhaustion was catching up" → "Exhaustion dragged at him"
- "The chair was cold" → "Cold metal bit through his jeans"

### 4. Telling Instead of Showing

You *tell* us about emotions instead of showing physical reactions:
- "His exhaustion was catching up" — show trembling hands, blurred vision, lead in his limbs
- "Doogan felt uncertain" — show stumbling, hesitation, hand on wall for balance
- "Relief" — show shoulders dropping, breath releasing, tension leaving face

### 5. Missing Sensory Detail

These scenes take place in:
- Holding cell (smell, sound, texture of cinder block)
- Police station (burned coffee, fluorescent hum, chemical cleaner)
- Eddie's kitchen (cooking smells, coffee, morning light)
- Doogan's condo (shower heat, water pressure, steam)
- Restaurant (ambient noise, food smells, table texture)
- Country club (grass smell, sun heat, golf course sounds)
- Strip mall (taco smell, nail salon fumes, heat radiating from asphalt)

You mention some of these but don't make us *feel* them. King would give us three sensory details per location. You're giving us maybe one.

### 6. Emotional Distance

Doogan is experiencing one of the worst 48 hours of his life. He's been arrested for murder. He blacked out. Someone set him up. His business partner is paying $15k for a lawyer. He's exhausted. He's scared.

Where is that in the prose?

You *tell* us he's exhausted. You *tell* us he's worried. But we never feel it in his body or his thoughts.

King would put us *inside* Doogan's physical experience—the ache, the fog, the nausea, the fight to stay upright.

### 7. Dialogue Attribution

Your dialogue is mostly clean but you have some weak tags:
- "Martinez's voice sharpened just slightly" — cut "just slightly"
- "Finch said menacingly" — the adverb tells us what the dialogue should show
- "Martinez brought things back" — not clear what this means

Prefer "said" or action beats over adverbs.

### 8. Scene Endings (Buttons)

Several scenes end with summarizing or decision-making instead of action:
- Scene 4: "Tonight, he just needed to sleep." (Too soft)
- Scene 5: "Tonight, he just needed to sleep." (Repeated!)
- Scene 6: "Even if that meant winning a scavenger hunt..." (Throat-clearing)
- Scene 7: "And preferably before Martinez figured out they were still digging." (Setup, not button)

End scenes on action, complication, or cliffhanger. Not summary.

## Structural Issues (Fiction Kit Constitution)

### McDonald Opening Rule Violations

**Scene 3 (Eddie's Kitchen):**  
Scene 2 ends with Eddie saying "pancakes fix most things." Scene 3 opens with Eddie mid-sentence about pancakes. This is borderline but works because new information (the job) enters immediately.

**Scene 4 (Doogan's Condo):**  
Scene 3 ends with Baxter bringing the earpiece. Scene 4 opens with Doogan at home. Good jump. No violation.

**Scene 5 (Restaurant Meeting):**  
Scene 4 ends with Doogan needing sleep. Scene 5 opens with the Billings meeting. Good jump forward. No violation.

Overall: Mostly clean on McDonald Rule.

### POV Consistency

All scenes maintain tight POV (Doogan in 1, 4, 6; Eddie in 2, 3, 5, 7). No head-hopping. Good.

**However:** You need to push deeper into the POV character's *internal experience*. Third-person limited should feel like first-person with pronouns. We should be *in their head*, not watching them from outside.

### Character Voice Issues

**Eddie:** Voice is consistent—run-on sentences, problem-solving energy, slightly manic. Works.

**Doogan:** Voice is too neutral. He should have more personality in his internal narration. What's his rhythm? His humor? His darkness?

**Abby:** Limited dialogue but what's there works—sharp, direct, caring but not soft.

**Martinez:** Consistent—patient, methodical, quietly menacing. Good.

## Specific Revision Priorities

### Priority 1: Add Physical/Sensory Detail
Go through every scene and add three specific sensory details:
- What does the space smell like?
- What does the character's body feel like?
- What's one visual detail that's *specific* (not "nice" or "good")?

### Priority 2: Show Emotional State Through Body
Every time you write "he was exhausted" or "he felt worried," replace it with physical detail:
- Exhaustion = heavy limbs, blurred vision, jaw ache from clenching, stumbling
- Worry = tight chest, shallow breathing, pulse in throat, hands cold
- Fear = gut clench, sweat, heart hammering, mouth dry

### Priority 3: Cut Adverbs
Search document for -ly words. Cut 80% of them. Find stronger verbs.

### Priority 4: Strengthen Scene Endings
Rewrite the last line of scenes 4, 5, 6, 7. End on action or complication, not summary.

### Priority 5: Deepen POV in Emotional Moments
- Scene 1: Doogan's reaction to the missing wine bottle
- Scene 2: Doogan's reaction to $15k cost
- Scene 3: Conversation about being drugged
- Scene 4: Shower scene emotional break
- Scene 5: Doogan's exhaustion at restaurant

These moments need to *hurt*. Make us feel them.

## Line-Edit Examples

### Before:
"The exhaustion was catching up—bone-deep, the kind that came from adrenaline crashes and fluorescent lights and holding cells with no furniture. His body ached. His mind felt slow."

### After:
"Exhaustion dragged at him—bone-deep, the crash after too much adrenaline in a fluorescent box with nowhere to sit. His shoulders felt welded to his spine. Thoughts moved through mud."

---

### Before:
"Doogan looked at her. Abby's green-hazel eyes were steady, concerned but not pitying. She was the only person he knew who could needle him about a murder accusation while making pancakes and somehow make both feel like acts of friendship."

### After:
"Abby's eyes—green-hazel, steady—held his. No pity. Just the kind of concern that came from actually giving a shit. She could needle him about murder and flip pancakes and somehow both felt like friendship."

---

### Before:
"The morning air was warm, Santa Ana wind carrying the smell of fresh-cut grass and distant jasmine."

### After:
"Morning air, warm. Santa Ana wind brought cut grass and jasmine—somebody's sprinklers running overtime."

**Note:** These edits aim for King's rhythm—shorter sentences, active construction, sensory precision.

## Final Assessment

This is solid crime fiction writing. Professional. Clean. Functional.

But it's not *alive* yet.

You're writing competent prose about a guy in trouble. King would write about what it *feels like* to be that guy—the body breaking down, the mind racing, the sensory overload or numbness, the terror underneath the competent surface.

Your dialogue is good. Your pacing is good. Your plot is good.

Now make the language *sing*. Make the reader feel Doogan's exhaustion in their own bones. Make the holding cell *stink*. Make the shower water *burn*. Make Eddie's panic *real*.

You've got the skeleton. Now put muscle on it.

**Recommended Next Steps:**
1. Rewrite Scene 4 (Doogan's Condo) with full sensory/emotional depth
2. Revise Scene 1 (Holding Cell) to add physical grounding
3. Cut every adverb that isn't load-bearing
4. Add three sensory details per scene
5. Rewrite scene endings (4, 5, 6, 7)

Then read the revised scenes aloud. Listen for rhythm. If it sounds like you're reading a police report, it needs more personality. If it sounds like you're *in the room*, you're getting there.
